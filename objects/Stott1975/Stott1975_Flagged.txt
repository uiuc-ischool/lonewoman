Each year, with the increase in number of children's books, it is often necessary to retreat from the volume of present publication to reexamine those works which have, for various reasons, endured to become classics. One such work is Scott O'Dell's <emph>__text__Island of the Blue Dolphins</emph>, Newbery award winner for __date__1958.

Although the desert island motif has been a standard fictional theme since Shakespeare's <emph>__text__Tempest</emph> and Defoe's <emph>__text__Robinson Crusoe</emph>, __person__O'Dell is faced with several new problems. Because he is writing children's fiction, he must create a story in which narrative pacing is relatively fast. His specific subject matter, the __date__lonely eighteen years spent by __person__Karana on __place__the Island of the Blue Dolphins, raises difficulties not experienced by the writer of another juvenile desert-island classic, __person__Armstrong Sperry in <emph>__text__Call it Courage</emph>. In the latter story, __person__the young hero's voyage is of short duration, and a constant suspense is maintained by the reader's awareness that __person__Mafatu must prove himself in time to escape from the cannibalistic enemies of his tribe. For a large part of her story, __person__Karana does very little except engage in the diurnal chores of survival. How, then, has __person__O'Dell created a story which continues to grip young readers __date__fourteen years after __date__its publication?

First, the story of Karana's isolation does not begin until the end of the eighth chapter, after her brother __person__Ramu has been killed by the pack of wild dogs. By so delaying the story of her survival, __person__O'Dell is able to create a sense of the social milieu in which she had developed, a feeling of the fear and distrust of the __org__Aleuts, which she will harbor during her solitude, and a contrast between the activity she had known with __org__the tribe and the desolation she faces alone. Moreover, the basic character traits she exhibits during her __date__eighteen years of loneliness have been clearly established during the early part of the novel.

Second, __person__O'Dell intermingles accounts of Karana's day-to-day activities with the highlights of her adventures. Thus the narrative pace is never allowed to slacken, while at the same time the reader is given a sense of her day-by-day existence. For example, Chapter Nine, which describes her first year alone, presents both her finding food and shelter and her fear of the roving dog pack; and Chapter Seventeen tells of repairing her canoe and learning to love __person___Rontu.

These "how to" selections are rendered interesting not only because they are placed between narrative segments, but also because of the vividness with which __person__O'Dell has presented them. Young readers have an interest in survival techniques, as is indicated by their own attempts to build tree houses and woods shelters. But too often this interest is catered to by such __org__Hollywood claptrap as "__television__Gilligan's Island." __person__O'Dell succeeds so well because of his deep knowledge of his subject. A native of the __place__California coast, he undoubtedly has dried abalone, hunted for devil fish, and learned to handle the offshore tides. Thus the convincing quality of his accounts renders them believable.

But the most important aspect of the story, that which has made it the classic it is, is the portrayal of the character of __person__Karana. Prefatory to an examination of her character, we should note two aspects. First, __person__O'Dell very wisely chooses the first person point of view. While Karana's life is interesting, her attitude to that life is much more so. Second, as attitudes are intangible, they must be given objective correlatives in order to be fictionally realized. Thus __text__the book is developed around a series of presentations of Karana's attitudes toward her daily survival activities, inanimate objects, animals, and other people. Each of the chapters thus contains a series of symbolic episodes which illuminate aspects of Karana's character and the changes it undergoes.

Chapters One through Eight present two main character traits which are fully explored in the remainder of __text__the novel: Karana's sociality within her family and __org__tribe and her fear of __org__the Aleuts. First seen gathering roots with her brother __person__Ramu, who has been her charge since the death of their mother, __person__Karana appears as a person fully involved in the life of __org__the tribe. She cheerfully accepts her duties as babysitter, acquiesces to the tribal taboos which involve sexual role separation, busily contributes to the arduous task of survival on the __place__somewhat barren island and happily bedecks herself, as does her sister __person__Ulape, in the maiden fashions of her people. In these early chapters, __person__O'Dell introduces several incidents and motifs which foreshadow and prepare the reader for incidents and themes to be later developed. __text__The novel opens with the arrival of the __place__Aleuts' ship and sets the scene for a series of departures from and returns to __place__the island. The remainder of __text__the book, which deals with Karana's psychic and physical survival and her final departure, can be divided into three major sections, each one tracing a significant phase in her character development. In each section there are a series of symbolic incidents which reveal aspects of this movement.

On first finding herself alone, Karana's hope is that she will soon be rescued by a returning ship. However, after a year, her hopes are ended and she realizes that she must take definite action herself, which she does by repairing a deserted canoe with which to paddle to a new island. In the early stages of her life on __place__the island, she has made two significant steps which have led to this position of self-reliance. Second, she has understood that if she is to survive and avenge her brother's death by wild dogs, she must violate the tribal taboo which forbids women to make weapons. The task is a difficult one, and, after much trial and error making bows and arrows, she decides that to be truly secure, she must make a spear of sea-elephant tusk.
