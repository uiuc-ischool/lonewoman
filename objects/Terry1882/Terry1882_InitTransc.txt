of <hi rend="underline">George Nidever</hi> and <hi rend="underline">Charles Brown</hi> of the Early History of Santa Barbara and the Fining [Finding] of the Woman (Indian) on San Nicholas Island.

Q. What is your name?

A. My name is George Nidever. I was born in the St<del rend="strikethrough">/</del>ate of Tennessee in 1802.

Q. How old were you when you left home?

A. I was a boy, quite small, not more than 10 years old, when I left Tennessee and went to Arkansas and Mexico.

Q. What did you do when you were in Arkansas and Mexico?

A. <del rend="strikethrough">Folled</del>Followed hunting. Traveled about from the time I was 18 years old and have been traveling ever since. I went to Texas and came back to Arkansas. There was a company coming to the Rocky Mts. in 1830. We traveled in Arkansas, given by treaty to the Cherokee Indians. I had to leave.

Q. What was your father's occupation?

A. Farmer

Q. That means hunter in those days?

A. Yes - small farmer - not like they do now.

Q. What started you to come to California?

A. It was a company coming to the Rocky Mountains for two years to trap beaver. I was a good shot and they wanted me to come out.

Q. What did you come out here to do?

A. The company were nearly all killed. Capt. Walker was coming across and they did not make much and said they were coming to California. Well I came with them.

Q. How many where there in the company?

A. Thirty-six when they came here.

Q. How many started? Ans. 48.

<del rend="strikethrough">A</del>Q. What become of the rest? Ans. Some stopped.

Q.Did you meet any Indians on the way? Ans. Yes.

Q. Did you have any trouble with them?

A. We were troubled all the while with them till we came to California. There was some trouble here but not with the Indians, but Indians from the north.

Q. Did you have any fights with them? Ans. Yes.

Q. Did you shoot at them? Ans. Yes, we killed 34 in one place.

Q. What tribe? Ans. Piutes.

Q. Did they kill any of your folks? Ans. No, they did not.

Q. You had guns and they had bows and arrows? Ans. Yes, and spades.

Q. What year was that when you came here?

A. 1834. Came into Monterey first. Struck the coast there at St. Joseph. Monterey was the capital. Struck the coast at a place called Gilroy.

Q. Packed, did you? Ans. Yes.

Q. Did you walk a good deal of the way? Ans. Yes, we had to kill our meat all the way.

Q. Did you live on meat all the way? Ans. Nothing but meat. Did not take any flour. 

Q. Was there any time when you could not find any game?

A. There was a time when we could not find much, but always killed something. 

Q. What did you kill - what kind of meat? Ans. Buffalo, elk, deer, bear.

Q. How long were you coming from Tennessee here?

A. I left about 10 years old. It [I] got here in '34  - several years on the way. Sometimes we would be stopped 2 or 3 years in a place trapping; catched (cached?) a good many beaver but lost them by the Indians.

Q. What did they do? Ans. Took the skins. They were worth $4.00 a lb.

Q. When you got here did you find any Americans?

A. Some Americans in 1834, about 50 or 60 in the country.

Q. How many Indians did you find here? Ans. More than white men.

Q. Ask him if he remembers anything about the old Missions. Tell us anything you remember about it.

A. They had cattle and horses. The priests had charge of everything; raised corn and wheat; raised enough to live on; had a good many cattle and lived on them.

Q. What did you do when you came here?

A. Hunted sea otter 20 or 30 years. It must be learned; we had no rifles like we have now, but one-barrel old fashioned rifle.

Q. How mahy Indians did you ever kill?

A. I could not tell how many. I know I killed four. There were only 36 of us; we had got well used to the Indians. We wanted to get them on us. Finally they followed us and finally they intended to take us. We did not know any names and that was called Mary Run, Salt Lake, and there was a trail and the trail went through a bunch of willows. We knew they intended to take us and at about 200 yards we turned off, and as soon as we turned off they came out. They l<del rend="strikethrough">k</del>ived in villages. Every village <del rend="strikethrough">g</del>has its captain. They came up. The first party coing up we met them, 15 of us, and about 200 yards away we tied the horses altog<del rend="strikethrough">o</del>ether.  There were 15 of us and these 15 were choice shots. They came walking right up within 40 or 50 yards of us and they were kind of falling to - every fellow had his hands full of arrows - and we commenced firing all 15 shots at once. There were 34 Indians killed, the rest ran away. You were asking me how many I killed. About two days a fterward [afterward] we were out, me and the captain. There were five. I got off to one side to the left and walked up to a place where we were to meet. They had not come yet. I saw two Indians coming. I thought they were after me, but they had not seen me. They ran along and kept looking back. I thought they had shot a man and so I thought I would kill one. I had a brother killed by them. Finally they came to a point of the hill. I shot. I killed them both. They just fell and rolled.

Q. Where about did you hit them?

A. The first was shot on the left side and killed instantly.

Q. Ask him if he did not see any friendly Indians, any he had confidence in.

A. No, could not trust any. There was what is called the Snake Indians, about the best, but could not trust them.

Q. Ask him if he had anything to do with the Apache Indians.

A. Yes, coming through New Mexico. Saw some there.

Q. Were not they friendly? A. Not very. They would steal.

The ship California was there and was coming down to Santa Barbara and saw some Americans and told them they ought to come here. When I came here it was in 1835 in the city of Santa Barbara. There were 17 or 18 at first the year I came to Santa Barbara, 85.

Q. This womn - how came she to be left on the Island?

A. Sparks took them all down to the beach, She had a child back on the land and wanted to go back and finally they let her go and she did not come back and they went away and she was left there. Sparkes came and said there was a woman left on the island. All expected she could not live.

Q. Who was Sparkes and how came to to go for the Indians?

A. The Mexicans sent them to bring them off. The schooner got lost just afterwards. They calculated to go back. All thought that the woman would not live long, so they did not go back. The schooner went to San Francisco and got lost.

Q. Why did you want to bring the Indians off anyway?

A. They wanted them at the Mission anyhow and the Government sent to fetch them off.

Q. Did the Indians go to work in the Mission, these particular ones?

A. They sent them to work different places, those they brought off.

Q. How did the Indians get on there in the first place?

A. Born and raised there - never been on the coast.

Q. Did you see the Indians on there before they were taken off?

A. No, I never saw them.

Q. How came you to find this woman was left there? Did you see her tracks?

A. Yes, it was three times before I found her. I went over. The priests here wanted me to hunt for her.

A. What priest was it? A. Father Gonzales, after Sanchez.

She had bone needles with eyes drilled in and she had a  kn<del rend="strikethrough">o</del>ife made with bone and another with a piece of wood, that she had when I catched her cleaning the sealskin. She had the rope nicely twisted with sinews and had it twisted as true as any ropemake could make, and she had bottles made of grass about the size of a gallon demijohn. We found some dishes of wood with handles.

Q. What became of all these things?

A. I gave them to Nidever. If we had taken care of the woman and shown her we could have made a hatful [hat full] of money. Captain of the steamer gave news that we had caught a wild woman.

Q. Was she baptized here? A. Yes, at the Mission.

Q. Do you know the name they gave her. A. Maria, something.

Q. They could not find out what her Indian nam  was?

A. No. Juana Maria I think she was named. The priests tried hard to find her life story, but she broke off into gibberish nobody could understand. Had Indians from Santa Barbara, Ventura, Santa Inez - they could not understand. Understood some words but not right through.

Q. Did she seem quite happy?

A. Happy as a lady. Shw [She]was dancing when anybody came. Then she <del rend="strikethrough">s</del>lift up her dress and made signs that I made her dress. While I was making her dress she tried to sew too. I had to thread her needle. She took this hand and put it through and handed it through like a shoemaker. She was content enough, if they had kept her from green corn, pumpkins and squash. Old Man Nidever told me he thought she would die the day before. She thought she was going to die. Nidever thought she would die that night when she came from the Island. I took baskets that was hanging. She got up from her bed the night before she went away and took a basket from the sycamore tree and threw the feathers out. One of the new baskets she gave to me. She lived two days after we left (Nidever and myself for San Francisco).

Q. I don't see what objection there could have been to take and show her.

A. Made money for her. People gave her money here. She did not know what money was. Clothes tickled her. She was only used to birdskin coats. I suppose it was often cold weather and she put the feathers next toher [to her] skin. On the mainland, if they had worked it right and kept her on her food she might have been alive yet. She was about 45 or 50 when we found her, because she walked with a kind of stoop. Singular part of it, probably seen people before. She made signs that she had seen men go  b<del rend="strikethrough">t</del>y.

Q. Did she give you any idea that she had seen the men before?

A. No. Ships going that way and this, but nobody came for her. 

Q. Had she anything rough over her head?

A. Nothing whatever. She held up her hand that way when the Indians came up and they let her know if she did run they would catch her.

Q. Did you say she spoke some words "Kanake?"

A. Yes. From one of the men that had the littls schooner named "Williams." She got the name <del rend="strikethrough">N</del>Better than Nothing."

Q. Mr. Nidever thought the priests hired them to go and get the Indians off.

A. I don't think so. Maybe so. I came here in 1844. The priests had all the control.

Q. What was the old Presidio? A. Barracks for the soldiers.

Q. How was it you reenlisted in an English ship?

A. Left the Prussian ship.
