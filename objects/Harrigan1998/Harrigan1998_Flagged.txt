When she returned they proceeded walking on to the beach. She understood their signs telling her to get into the boat. After boarding, she went to the bow and sat down, placing one hand on each side of the boat.

Arriving at __place__the schooner, __person__the woman climbed aboard and went to the galley where she made signs to indicate that it was warm and it seemed to please her. At dinner time __person__the crew gave __person__the woman some of their food which she ate without any signs that it disagreed with her.<hi rend="supsercript">56</hi>

That afternoon, __person__Dittman made __person__the woman her first "civilized" clothes in order to preserve the unusual cormorant feather gown she was wearing. Her new outfit consisted of a petticoat made of ticking, a man's shirt, a black necktie and an old cape which __person__Nidever gave to her. While __person__Dittman was making her petticoat she laughed at his method of sewing. About this time, __person__Nidever gave her the cape which was badly in need of mending. Since her eyes seemed to be weak, she could not see to thread the needle and __person__Dittman threaded it for her so that she could mend the cape. Her method was rather unusual. She placed the cloth between her legs, then thrust the needle through the cloth with one hand and then pulled the needle the remainder of the way with the other hand. In spite of its crudeness, her method proved to be successful. She was very pleased with her new clothes and wanted everyone to notice her wearing them.<hi rend="supsercript">57</hi>

The next day __person__the crew went ashore in the same location to set up a camp for otter hunting. They put up a tent for themselves and a hut-type shelter for __person__the woman. They remained on __person__the island for nearly a month. During this time, __person__the men were gone nearly all day, but __person__the woman kept busy collecting firewood and water for the camp. She did this willingly and without ever being asked. She always wanted to help in any way that she could. While working, she talked, laughed and sang. If she wasn't helping out in the camp she was weaving baskets. She worked on several baskets at one time, going from one to the other. As a result, she never finished any that she had started.<hi rend="supsercript">58</hi>

__person__The woman seemed to like the type of food __person__the men prepared. Only once did she show any sign of returning to her old source of food. On this occasion __person__Dittman killed a female otter and dragged it ashore to skin it. After it was skinned he was going to dispose of the carcass. __person__The woman stopped him and scolded him for the waste of meat. She dragged the carcass up the beach and left it. After a few days, however, the carcass began to smell, and when __person__the crew went to dispose of it, __person__the woman made no objections. In fact, __person__Dittman says, she made signs to get rid of it because of the smell.<hi rend="supsercript">59</hi>

The otter was within a few days of giving birth when it had been shot. The unborn otter was removed from the female, skinned and stuffed, and given to __person__the woman. She hung it by a string in her shelter and played with it for hours, hitting it to keep it in motion.<hi rend="supsercript">60</hi>

Having completed their hunt, __person__the group packed to leave __person__the island. After their departure, such a violent storm arose that they thought they would have to return to __place__the island for safety. During the storm __person__the woman made signs that she could stop the wind. She knelt down on the deck facing the wind and seemed to be praying. She did this a number of times during the day, and when the wind stopped that afternoon, she smiled and made signs that her prayers had been answered. The night was spent on the lee side of __place__Santa Cruz Island and the next morning they set out for the __place__Santa Barbara mainland.<hi rend="supsercript">61</hi>

As they sailed into __place__Santa Barbara harbor, __person__the woman saw an ox-cart on the shore. She pointed to it and danced and laughed the entire time it was in sight. One of Nidever' s sons had come on horseback to greet them as they came ashore and __person__the woman ran around the horse pointing, laughing and dancing.<hi rend="supsercript">62</hi>

After his arrival in __place__Santa Barbara, __person__Nidever took __person__the woman to stay at __place__his home. While she was there she often went into __place__town, but she never came home without some gifts for __person__the children. Many people stopped at __person__Santa Barbara to see her; some brought gifts which she accepted but did not open. After the people had gone, she would take great delight in giving the gifts to __person__the children of the house. __person__The woman was always in good spirits and sang and danced when people came.<hi rend="supsercript">63</hi>

In an effort to find someone who could speak the woman's language, __org__the Mission Fathers sent for __org__the Indians from surrounding areas. It is not clear if they were unable to find a translator or, if those who came could not converse with her. It is not unlikely that, over the period of eighteen years, she had developed a language of her own. She must have heard the language of the hunters who came to __place__the island during those the [sic] years, because she used such words as <emph>pickininy</emph> in referring to her child, and <emph>manana</emph>.<hi rend="supsercript">64</hi>

In sign language, __person__the woman told __person__Nidever and others that, "....she did not find her child, that she wandered for days without tasting any food or drink, sometimes sleeping but [a] little, until her clothes were torn, and her feet and legs bleeding."<hi rend="supsercript">65</hi>

The fact that she did not find her child is recorded in several different accounts by __person__Nidever, and it can be assumed to be true. The stories about her finding __person__the child and its being partially devoured by the dogs came from sources that have proved to be erroneous in several instances. The fact that __person__the woman did not find her child would tend to discount the claim of __person__Arthur R. Sanger of __place__Los Angeles who says that he has the skeleton of __person__the child.<hi rend="supsercript">66</hi>

With regard to __person__the child, __person__Nidever continues in his report:

<emph>After a time she forgot her child and sang and danced. She also told that she was very sick at one time; that she had seen vessels passing two [sic] and fro but none came to take her off; that she saw us on __place__the island before we found her. ... About 5 weeks after she was brought over, she was taken sick from eating too much fruit and 7 weeks from the day of her arrival died.</emph><hi rend="supsercript">67</hi>

While __person__the Indian woman was ill, __person__Mrs. Nidever cared for her. She cooked some seal blubber for her but __person__the woman would not eat it and pointed to her worn teeth. Before she died, the __org__Mission Fathers baptized her <emph>sub conditione</emph> (conditionally, because they did not know if she had been baptized before) and named her __person__Juana Maria. She was buried in __place__the Mission cemetery on __date__October 19, 1853. The burial record is Number 1183 on Folio 13 of the Register of Burials of the __org__Presidio of Santa Barbara.

The woman's possessions were great curiosities. One of her two water-tight baskets was given to some __person__unknown person who reported this along with a picture of the basket to <emph>__org__Hutchings' Californa Magazine</emph>.<hi rend="supsercript">68</hi> Her dress and several other articles were reportedly sent to Rome's __place__Vatican Museum, but there have been no reliable reports of their ever having been seen. The remainder of her things, and probably the dress, were sent to __place__San Francisco, where they were lost in the __date__1906 earthquake.<hi rend="supsercript">69</hi>

In __date__1928 a plaque was erected on the church wall in the __place__Santa Barbara Mission Cemetery. It reads as follows:

It is almost beyond the grasp of a normal person to imagine how a human being could be left alone for eighteen years on an island and still remain as happy and cheerful as did __person__Juana Maria and all of the time hoping and waiting for her rescue.

<hi rend="bold">Epilogue</hi>

One might wonder what has happened to __person__Juana Maria and to __place__San Nicolas Island since __date__1853. No one knows where the remains of __person__Juana Maria, or most others, are buried in the __place__Santa Barbara Mission Cemetery. The practice of that day was to dig up the bones of all of those in an area and re-bury them in a common grave when the cemetery filled up. For this reason, the plaque to __person__Juana Maria is on the wall of __place__the Church in the __place__Mission Cemetery and not on any particular plot of ground.

At one time or another, sheep were run on many of __place__the Channel Islands, totally destroying any natural vegetation on __place__the islands. As a result, they are slowly being worn away. In a conversation with the Navy's Ecologist, __person__Mr. Ron Dow, in __date__October of 1982, I learned that there were no otter on __place__San Nicolas Island, but there were large numbers of sea lions and sea elephants. __place__The island is indeed wearing away and __person__Mr. Dow and others spend considerable time investigating areas where the elements have exposed burial sites from the __org__Indian inhabitants. Once the sites are researched, the remains are carefully re-buried where, it is hoped, they will remain undisturbed by the elements in future years.

<hi rend="bold">__place__San Nicolas Island Today</hi>

__place__San Nicolas is one of the eight __place__Channel Islands off the __place__southern California coast and contains over 20 square miles of gently rolling terrain accented with steep sea cliffs. These steep cliffs are perfect nesting habitat for seabirds such as cormorants and western gulls. Atop __place__the island the tiny island fox reigns as the largest land mammal. The isolated beaches provide resting places for three species of pinnipeds (seals and sea lions)--northern elephant seals, harbor seals and __place__California sea lions.

Dense undersea kelp forests surround __place__the island providing food and shelter for many species of fish, invertebrates, and once again--for sea otters. Pristine tidepools ring the island's rocky shores where crabs, abalone, sea urchins and sea snails have adapted to their ever-changing homes. __place__San Nicolas Island is typical of the islands in the __place__Channel Islands archipelago--but it is also unique.

<hi rend="bold">A Military Presence</hi>

Since __date__1933, __place__San Nicolas Island has been under the jurisdiction of the __org__U. S. Navy. Approximately 400 people work and staff __place__the island. __place__San Nicolas is part of the Pacific Missle Test Center's Sea Test Range. Navy personnel work on projects such as missle tracking and weapon system testing. A 10,000 foot runway accomodates supersonic target aircraft and planes from the __place__mainland during operations. Facilities for island personnel include a bowling alley, cafeteria and a hobby shop. Because of the military operations, visitation by the general public is prohibited.

The natural resources of __place__San Nicolas Island are managed by a joint agreement between the __org__Department of the Navy, the __org__Department of the Interior and __org__California's Department of Fish and Game.

*****
The __org__U. S. Fish and Wildlife Service is currently working on a sea otter recovery program on __place__the island. Once abundant around all the __place__Channel Islands, sea otters were hunted to extinction for their valuable pelts. Biologists working on the sea otter project relocate animals from the __place__Monterey Bay population in hopes that a colony of otters will be established once again in __place__Southern California.
