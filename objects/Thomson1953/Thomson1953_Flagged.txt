Note on __person__George Nidever

<emph>A "Clean-Living and Upright" Trapper</emph>

<emph>By</emph> __person__VIRGINIA THOMSON

IN HIS __text__"Answer to One Reader's Complaint about <emph>__text__The Big Sky</emph>," __person__William Sloane, the book's original publisher, made the following statement from __place__New York City in __date__1947, with regard to __person__Alfred B. Guthrie:

The purpose of __person__the author has been to show that in living and acting as they did (hunters and trappers), they destroyed the wilderness, which was the only thing they really loved. I don't see how __person__the author could have written the story&#8212;and it is a very moral story&#8212;that he wanted to tell if __person__his hero had been a clean-living, upright man. There weren't any clean-living, upright men engaged in despoiling the American wilderness at that time.

The purpose of the present article is to point out that there was at least one "clean-living, upright man" engaged in trapping at that time, namely, __person__George Nidever, a native of __place__Tennessee, whose experiences in "despoiling [?] the American wilderness" did not interfere with his becoming, and remaining, a respected citizen of __place__Santa Barbara for half a century.

As a young man, not wishing to pursue the comparatively static life of a farmer like his father, __person__George Nidever had chosen the opposite one of hunting and trapping. While out after beaver with a party under __person__Col. Robert Bean in __date__1830-33, he explored areas that are now parts of __place__New Mexico, __place__Wyoming, __person__Montana, and __person__Idaho.

__person__Nidever was no common trapper. He was a marksman of such great ability that companies of trappers were said to have bet as high as $5,000 that __person__Nidever could kill more buffalo than any other man in the __place__Rocky Mountains. While he was living in __place__California, he became renowned as a grizzly bear hunter; according to reports, he killed more than 200. Sea otters on the __place__coast of California succumbed to his marksmanship to the recorded number of 1091, between the years __date__1835 and __date__1848. <hi rend="superscript">1</hi> Even when he was in his seventies, his skill did not desert him. On __date__September 1, 1878, __person__E. F. Murray, an assistant of __person__Hubert Howe Bancroft, visited __person__Nidever in the vicinity of __place__Santa Barbara and took down his autobiography; he also wrote a description of him at 75 years of age:

The subject of this sketch. . . . is still strong and active. He is about the medium height and inclined to be stout. He stoops the least bit but it may be from habit rather than old age. His sight and hearing are still keen and his nerves remarkably steady for one so old. He lives with his __person__youngest daughter and his __person__wife, about a mile from __place__town, on a piece of land containing several acres. It is under cultivation and he keeps it free from squirrels by shooting them with a Colt's revolver. If a chicken is wanted for dinner he prefers shooting its head off with his rifle to using a shot gun, which he has frequently to use, however, owing to the danger of using his rifle when there are so many and near neighbors. <hi rend="superscript">2</hi>

__person__Murray induced __person__Nidever to shoot three shots at a paper target, three inches square, hanging on a nail some sixty yards away. All three shots landed on the paper, the second shot hitting the nail. __person__Nidever used a muzzle-loader in a resting position.

__person__William Henry Ellison speaks of another merit that __person__Nidever had as a trapper; this was a "remarkable ability to recognize different Indian tribes and to remember their location." <hi rend="superscript">3</hi> As will be seen later, his shrewdness on one occasion saved his own and others' lives.

Physical difficulties never seemed to bother __person__Nidever. While he was with __person__Colonel Bean's party, dense timberlands were encountered and 12 to 16 inches of snow in the mountain gorges of __place__Wyoming. After six days without food, __person__Nidever shot and killed two buffalo bulls. __person__The men were so hungry that they continued to eat the flesh of one bull all night, until six o'clock the next morning. According to __person__Job Dye, a member of __person__the party, ". . . . they would skin a small piece and cut off a chunk of meat and throw it into the fire, and there let it remain until it became black and charred, when it was taken from the smoking blaze and eaten, almost as raw as when it was alive in the buffalo hide." <hi rend="superscript">4</hi>

In his own account and in the accounts of others who knew him, I have been unable to find that __person__George Nidever used any obscene language or profanity, or that he was disrespectful toward God and man. He killed Indians only in self-defense, and not for scalps. Nor did he steal horses and boats.

He lived among the toughest of men, but he was not like them in his morals. An example of one of these "toughest of men" was __person__Isaac Graham, whom __person__Theodore H. Hittell describes as "a man entirely without education," but possessed of "enterprise and intelligence" and "a considerable amount of personal magnetism." <hi rend="superscript">5</hi> He was a close companion of __person__Nidever at first. But their friendship ceased when __person__Graham became leader of a motley crew, who helped put __person__Alvarado in office and then sought to take abusive advantage of __person__the governor.

In __date__1844-__date__1845, when __person__José Castro joined in a revolt to drive __person__Gov. Manuel Micheltorena out of __place__California, __person__Nidever refused to join the latter's foreign company. Even though he favored __person__Micheltorena, __person__Nidever had his scruples: many of the soldiers were freed convicts, and the characters of officers and regulars questionable.

There was one incident in Nidever's life, of which he repented later. While with __person__Colonel Bean's company in __date__1830, "below the __place__mouth of the Cimmeron," <hi rend="superscript">6</hi> __person__Nidever started out with __person__Isaac Graham to get buffalo. They walked a full four miles from camp before they came upon any, and, when they did, the __person__two men, being on foot, had no trouble in approaching unnoticed. But they realized that they had walked into a trap when 80 armed __org__Arapaho Indians appeared from nowhere and began chasing them. The nearest timber was fully a mile and a half away, over by __place__the river. They started running for their lives. __person__Graham, the better athlete, could have left __person__Nidever in the rear, but "it was not his character to desert a comrade in danger." <hi rend="superscript">7</hi> Not until nearly a mile of ground had been covered did their pursuers catch up with __person__the white men; then, to their surprise, __org__the Indians threw down their own guns and proceeded to give the __person__two men a rough shaking. By signs, __person__Nidever and __person__Graham made them understand that there were 80 men in the camp of the white men&#8212;a slight exaggeration. <hi rend="superscript">8</hi> This scheme had its desired effect, for __org__the Indians spared the white men's lives and even accompanied them back to their camp. As will be seen, it didn't take long for the __org__Arapahoes to realize that they had been tricked.

__person__Colonel Bean decided to send out six men to look for winter quarters and game, and, when volunteers were asked for, __person__Isaac Graham, __person__George Nidever, __person__James Basey, __person__Mark Nidever (George's younger brother), __person__Job Dye, and __person__Frederick Christ were selected. <hi rend="superscript">9</hi> __person__The six traveled about 60 miles before they found a beautiful valley, apparently made-to-order for a winter camp, in the vicinity of the __place__Arkansas River. __person__Mark Nidever and __person__Frederick Christ were sent back to inform the __person__main company and guide them to __place__the location. Meanwhile the __person__other four set to work killing buffalo and curing the meat for the winter. According to __person__Dye, "In four days time we had killed and hung up seventeen buffalo and covered them with hides; also a large grizzly bear." <hi rend="superscript">10</hi> The meat was suspended beyond reach of wild animals, and __person__the men then moved their camp to a place on the side of the mountain, whence they could watch the meat and the valley below without being seen themselves.

On the fifth day, late in the evening, __person__George Nidever felt uneasy about the meat and decided to take a stroll outside camp. Carrying his rifle with him, he walked about 400 yards before he saw that a war party of about 80 __org__Arapahoes <hi rend="superscript">11</hi> were stealing the buffalo and bear meat. He reported his findings to his __person__companions, and all agreed to abandon camp and look for __person__Colonel Bean's main company. When they reached it, the first thing that greeted __person__Nidever was the news that the two men sent back&#8212;his brother __person__Mark, and __person__Frederick Christ&#8212;had been killed by __org__the Indians. They had reached camp in safety and were acting the part of guides for the return journey, when they met the 75 to 80 __org__Arapahoes who had been stealing the buffalo meat. The __person__main company knew that the __person__two men had reached the place selected for winter-quarters, because they found their names cut in the bark of a log on which they had sat before being taken captive. <hi rend="superscript">12</hi> __org__Their captors were the same __org__Arapahoes who had chased __person__George Nidever and __person__Isaac Graham; realizing that they had been tricked, __org__the Indians had taken revenge on __person__two men not at all connected with the original deception. Later on, __person__George met some Mexicans. He recognized that one of them had his brother's gun. When questioned, the man revealed that __org__the Indians had sold Mark's gun to him, and Christ's to another member of the __person__Mexican party. Also gleaned was the fact that __person__Mark had killed three of __org__the Indians before they killed him.

__person__Nidever said of his brother that he ". . . . was one of the best men we had in our company&#8212;active, strong, hardy, brave, and a good shot. He was missed and mourned by all. " <hi rend="superscript">13</hi>

__person__Nidever could not shake the picture of his brother's death from his mind. Three years afterward, he was a member of __person__Capt. Joseph Reddeford Walker's party&#8212;the first party known to have crossed the __place__Sierra Nevada going west into __place__California. Just after entering the mountains, __person__Captain Walker chose __person__George Nidever and __person__Zenas Leonard to help him select a camping-place. They decided to take different routes. It was not long before __person__Nidever discovered fresh signs of Indians. Alarmed not only for his own safety, but also for the safety of __person__Walker and __person__Leonard, he was looking around for more signs, when he happened to turn in the direction from which he had come and saw two Indians trotting along the trail, with their heads down. It was evident that they had seen __person__Leonard and __person__Walker and had fled from the scene, for every few yards they would stop, look back, and listen as if pursued.

At first __person__Nidever thought that they were following his own tracks, and, going behind a tree, he prepared to meet them. But the Indians were not even aware that he existed, for they passed in single file a few feet from him, conversing as they went. He decided to let them go; then the brutal mangling of his brother came to his mind: ". . . . watching my chance, just before they would have to turn around a small point of rocks, I fired, shooting both of them dead at the first shot." __person__Zenas Leonard says that __person__Nidever killed the Indians because he thought that they had already attacked __person__Walker and him; that __person__Nidever was sorry when he found out his mistake.

In describing Nidever's character, __person__E. F. Murray, __person__Bancroft's assistant, wrote: "He is quite unassuming and never brags of his feats of skill, and almost everything of this nature relating to him I first learned from others, obtaining a recital of them from him only by dint of questioning. His truthfulness and integrity are beyond question, so that great weight should be given to all he says." <hi rend="superscript">16</hi>
