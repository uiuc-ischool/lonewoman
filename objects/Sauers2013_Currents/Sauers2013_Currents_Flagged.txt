<hi rend="bold">__date__LATE LAST YEAR</hi>, __person__Navy archaeologist Steven Schwartz discovered a hidden cave on __place__San Nicolas Island which he believes may be the Lone Woman’s.

For more than __date__20 years __person__Schwartz has been looking for the home of the __person__Lone Woman, the inspiration for __person__Scott O’Dell’s __date__1960 Newbery Medal-winning novel <emph>__text__The Island of the Blue Dolphins<emph>. The __text__book is based on the abandonment and retrieval of an __person__American Indian woman who lived alone on __place__San Nicolas Island for __date__18 years (from 1835 to 1853), and __person__Schwartz believes the cave he’s uncovered may answer the question that has long eluded researchers: “How did she live for those __date__18 years?”

There are a number of sources that discuss cave sites on __place__San Nicolas Island showing evidence of Indian occupation, but only one cave site had been discovered&#8212;the "__person__Cave of the Whales." The __person__Cave of the Whales contains pictographs and petroglyphs primarily depicting sea life such as killer whales. There is little evidence of occupation within the cave as this cave floor is located just above the high tide line, and during stormy weather waves would crash in making habitation unlikely.

During archaeological surveys conducted in recent decades, no cave site other than the __person__Cave of the Whales had been located. This led to the conclusion that if there was a cave where the __person__Lone Woman lived, it had either collapsed or been buried in the intervening decades.

During research into the ranching history of the __place__island, a map was located which clarified the question of a second cave site. A U.S. Coast and Geodetic Survey map by __person__Stehman Forney in the __date__summer of 1879 clearly shows the location of the "__place__Indian Cave." This cave is near, but definitely distinct from the __place__Cave of the Whales. The map shows a survey station, named “Cave,” using the normal symbol for a survey station, but near this, the annotation “__place__Indian Cave” without the survey station symbol appears to denote a specific place name. This map established the clear location of a second cave and erased all notions of the two caves being separate references to the same geographic feature.

During the __date__next 20 years, various attempts were made to locate the cave. The map, while detailed, did not aid in the search of the cave because of the complex nature of the shoreline and the scale of the map. 

“It was not possible to locate one specific spot as the location of the __place__Indian Cave,” __person__Schwartz said. “There were at least four topographic features in the immediate vicinity that could be evidence of a collapsed or buried cave.” 

Surface surveys and auger and shovel sampling were conducted at all four locations with no success.

A recently obtained copy of the field notes which accompany the __date__1879 U.S. Coast Survey map from __person__Scott Byram, a researcher at __org__University of California, Berkeley, provided a more specific location for the cave and precise distance and bearing from the “Cave” survey station to the “__place__Indian Cave” survey station.

With this very specific location and some help from __org__California State University, Los Angeles (CSLA) students, the cave was finally located in early __date__2012. This involved clearing vegetation and removing some recently deposited sediments that were filling the cave. Excavations proceeded down to where a rounded sandstone boulder was found, likely indicating the floor of the cave. At that point, work by the team stopped so as not to impact the deposit or begin excavation without the proper approvals. As the site was being cleaned up for photographs, a glass bottle was recovered from the bottom of the cleared area. The bottle is a Gothic-style condiment bottle that dates __date__between 1840 and 1865. Such a date would be consistent with the cave's use by the __person__Lone Woman.

The thought that the cave was actually the home of the __person__Lone Woman of San Nicolas Island for those __date__18 lonely years brought __person__Schwartz and the team to the question, “So what did she do for __date__18 years?” The team hopes this cave site will provide an answer once work resumes. 

“It’s very rare in archaeology to be able to pinpoint any find to a specific individual,” __person__Schwartz said. “To have such a narrow time period that you’re looking at is exactly what we need to pinpoint the context of these finds. It’s just something you never see around here.”

The cave is currently referred to as the “__place__Lost Indian Cave” due to the fact that its location was lost for many years. Once it can be firmly established that it was the home of the __person__Lone Woman of San Nicolas Island, it may then be referred to as the “__place__Lone Woman Cave.”
