<emph>By</emph> __person__TRAVIS HUDSON

IN A PREVIOUS ISSUE of __org__THE MASTERKEY (Vol. 52, No. 1; __date__January-March, 1978), I reported the exciting discovery of some ethnographic notes among John P. Harrington's papers which pertained to the __person__"Lone Woman" of San Nicolas Island. These materials were found in Boxes 4 and 745 of his __org__Chumash material culture and ritual notes, now housed in the __org__Smithsonian Institution. Recently I came across some additional material on this interesting segment of __place__California history in Box 747, which also pertains to Chumash ethnography. The edited notes are provided below. As with the first series of notes on __person__this woman, the informant is __person__Fernando Librado:

__person__Captain Nidever and an Indian named __person__Malquiares<hi rend="superscript">1</hi> found __person__a woman on __place__San Nicolas Island rolling silver<hi rend="superscript">2</hi> in her hands to make abalorio.<hi rend="superscript">3</hi> __person__Malquiares spoke to her at Captain Nidever's request. __person__Malquiares told __person__Fernando that when they encountered __person__the woman, she sang the following song:<hi rend="superscript">4</hi>

<emph>tokitoki</emph>

<emph>yamymna</emph>

<emph>tokitoki</emph>

<emph>weleleshkima</emph>

<emph>yaamymina</emph>

<emph>weleleshkima</emph>

<emph>yaamymina</emph>

<emph>tokitoki</emph>

__person__The woman sang these words. After hearing them __person__Malquiares could sing them, and they later became deeply impressed on Fernando's memory. Once, when __person__Fernando met __person__Aravio,<hi rend="superscript">5</hi> a __person__very aged Indian from __place__Santa Ines, __person__Fernando recited these words to him, and at once __person__Aravio translated the song for __person__Fernando. The words meant: "I leave contented, because I see the day when I want to get out of __place__this island."

When discovered the woman's only clothing consisted of a skirt made of feathers and an apron. __person__Jacob Nidever and his people were otter hunters. They made __person__the woman a dress out of their trousers. It was a month that they stayed on __place__the island before they found __person__the woman. After they found her, she would not eat their food, but she did stay around their camp. __person__Mr. L. A. M. Ortega<hi rend="supersceript">6</hi> says that he has heard that after __person__the woman was removed to __place__Santa Barbara, she was a great hand at eating tallow. __person__The woman was taken to the __place__house of Jacob Nidever's folks in __place__Santa Barbara, an adobe situated close to where the __place__Southern Pacific Freight Station now stands.<hi rend="superscript">7</hi> When __person__the men first put on the dress they had prepared for her out of their trousers, she began to sing and dance. She sang this song:<hi rend="superscript">8</hi>

<emph>hi(i)hihiyo'oo</emph> (twice)

<emph>kachnaualanalna'al</emph>

(swinging in the dance when this line was sung)

<emph>hihihiyo'oo</emph>

__org__The Indians made blankets, sometimes by using strips of grass woven together. They called this grass grama in Spanish; it has long roots. As late as &#8212; [they still made them?]; __person__Fernando still &#8212; &#8212; of this stuff and the sandal from the __person__San Nicolas woman.<hi rend="superscript">9</hi> She made her skirt of trousers and put a man's shirt on herself. After that [she, they?] threw away her Indian clothes. __person__The woman would go to get water at low tide and this was her drinking water. 

<hi rend="bold>End Notes</hi>

1. __person__Malquiares, a __org__Ventureńo Chumash, told __person__Fernando that he and __person__Hilario Valenzuela visited the __person__San Nicolas Island woman at the __place__Nidever home in __place__Santa Barbara (__person__Hudson __date__1978: 24). 2. Unclear what is being described. The silver may refer to the Mexican silver dollar or peso (__person__Hudson <emph>et al</emph>. __date__1978: 162, note 352).

3. Abalorio is shell bead money.

4. The song is somewhat similar, yet distinct, from one given to __person__Harrington by __person__Fernando before (__person__Hudson __date__1978: 25 and 28, note 14), whereby the person from whom __person__Fernando had learned it was unknown. We are now told that this song, and perhaps the other version of it, can be attributed to __person__Malquiares.

5. __person__Aravio is conceivably __person__Arabio <emph>Talawiyashwit</emph> (__person__"Imitator"), a Santa Cruz Island Chumash man who was a member of the <emph>__org__'antap</emph> religious cult, resident of __place__Santa Ines, and once performed the part of the coyote in a Chumash Devil Dance held at __place__Mission Santa Barbara (__person__Hudson <emph>et al</emph>. __date__1977: 28, 89-90). 6. __person__L.A. M. Ortega, one of Harrington's informants between __date__1912 and __date__1915, was probably __person__Luis Antonio Maria Ortega. Harrington's notes state that L. A. M. Ortega's father was __person__Antonio Maria Ortega, whereas in another note he stated the father's name was __person__Jose Manuel Ortega. __person__Harrington went on to add that the __person__Ortega family employed many Indians, and that they learned Chumash from contact with __person__these people. __person__Andres Ortega's mother, __person__Harrington wrote, talked the Refugio dialect of __org__Barbareńo Chumash very well.

7. Early maps of __place__Santa Barbara indicate that the depot was located approximately where it is now: the corner of __place__Chapala and __place__Yanonali, not far from __place__Burton Mound and the site of the adobe home of __person__Captain George Nidever (__person__Rogers __date__1929: 100).

8. The song appears to be another version of the one reported elsewhere (__person__Hudson __date__1978: 24); again we are not told who reported it to __person__Fernando but, based upon the context in which the first song was learned, it would appear that it was __person__Malquiares.

9. The blanks are Harrington's. What is meant by __person__Fernando in reference to the woman's sandals is unclear; the meaning may have been that the woman's sandals were made also from strips of grass woven together. A sandal, collected by __person__Lorenzo Yates and said to have been found in a cave on __place__Anacapa Island, is in the collections of the __org__Santa Barbara Museum of Natural History.
