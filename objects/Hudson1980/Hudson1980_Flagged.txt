<hi rend="bold">ADDITIONAL HARRINGTON NOTES ON THE __person__"LONE WOMAN"</hi>

<hi rend="bold"><emph>By</emph> __person__Travis Hudson</hi>

IN TWO PREVIOUS ISSUES OF __org__THE MASTERKEY (Vol. 52, Nos. 1 &amp; 4, __date__1978) several exciting discoveries of ethnographic notes pertaining to the __person__"Lone Woman" of __place__San Nicolas Island were reported. These notes were found among the extensive papers of __person__John P. Harrington for the __org__Ventureńo Chumash. Recently, however, two additional notes on the __person__"Lone Woman" have surfaced among Harrington's papers for the __org__Barbareńo Chumash. Interviewed in __place__Santa Barbara some time between __date__1912 and __date__1922, the informant in this case was __person__Luisa Ygnacio, a full-blood __org__Barbareńo Chumash who was baptized at __place__Mission Santa Barbara about __date__1830. __person__Harrington used the abbreviation "Sa." and "La." when referring to her (__person__Blackburn __date__1975: 19).

The first short note is as follows:

"__person__Sa. has never seen a shell fishhook, except the one that the __person__last woman of San Nicolas had. It was a big shell fishhook, and [the] fishline [with it] was as thick as a lead pencil. __person__Sa.'s spouse brought it.<hi rend="superscript">1</hi> __person__Sa. had it at __place__Sa.'s home on the ranch, but after a while it got thrown away. __person__Sa. also had at the ranch two feather capes belonging to the __person__last woman of San Nicolas which __person__Sa.'s spouse brought at the same time that he brought the fishhook.<hi rend="superscript">2</hi> These were small (not big) and were made by sewing together seagull skins with [the] feathers still on, __person__Sa. thinks. [The] feathers [were] on [the] outside [of the capes]. Used them [by wearing them] thrown over [the] shoulders. When __person__Sa.'s spouse died, __person__Sa. threw the capes and hook away.<hi rend="superscript">3</hi>"

The second note mentions these details concerning __person__the woman:

"They had the __person__last woman of San Nicolas Island here in __place__Santa Barbara. __person__Sa. did not go to see her, but many people did. __person__Martina went to see her, and either __person__Martina or others said she talked like a __org__Fernandino.<hi rend="superscript">4</hi> So they got __person__Pilar to come and try to talk with her.<hi rend="superscript">5</hi> __person__Pilar talked good __org__Barbareńo Chumash, but she never forgot her native tongue and talked it when the __org__Fernandinos came here to __place__Santa Barbara. __person__Pilar tried to talk with __person__the woman in Fernandino, but she said she could not understand.<hi rend="superscript">6</hi>

"If they had let __person__the woman stay with __org__the Indians here, she would have been all right. But they kept her with __person__Qorqe (__person__George Nidever). __person__Martina and __person__Pilar came from __place__Ventura one time and got some clams and maybe some other things on the road, and when they passed by here, in __place__Santa Barbara, they visited __person__the woman and gave her the clams. __person__The woman was greatly pleased. __person__Martina and __person__Pilar had just made a little trip to __place__Ventura. __person__Sa. thinks they had seen __person__the woman before that time."

<hi rend="bold">End Notes</hi>

1. In __date__1853, when the __person__"Lone Woman" was taken off __place__the island and brought to __place__Santa Barbara, __person__Luisa Ygnacio would have been about 23 years old. She married __person__Policarpio, also __org__Chumash, in __date__1851 (__person__Blackburn __date__1975: 19). According to other Harrington notes, __person__Policarpio was a fisherman residing in an Indian community of fishermen at __place__Goleta Estero, just west of __place__Santa Barbara. His father was __person__Jose Sudon <emph>Kamuliyatset</emph>, a __person__Santa Cruz Island Indian baptized on __date__July 29, 1819 at __place__Mission Santa Barbara. <emph>__person__Kamuliyatset</emph> was the captain of the __place__Goleta village where he resided and was also the owner of a Chumash plank canoe which was used in the Chumash Revolt of __date__1824 (__person__Hudson __date__1976).

2. A feather dress was reported for __person__the woman, described as made of several square-cut shag skins sown together (__person__Dittman __date__1973: 1; __person__Nidever __date__1973: 10). The dress was also described to __person__Harrington years later by a __person__Mr. and __person__Mrs. del Valle, __person__"old Santa Barbara residents," which in his notes is described as follows:

"They said that __person__the woman had an apron of fiber on underneath her duckskin cloak. The dress which she wore was of shag, not duckskins. Feathers were all around the hem. They all faced the same way. The skins were neatly joined. They said it was a beautiful piece of work. The dress had holes for the arms. It reached almost to her knees. The one she wore appeared not to be finished."

The dress was reportedly sent to __place__Rome by __person__Father Gonzalez Rubio of __org__Mission Santa Barbara. The description of the cape's manufacture is similar to that of the dress, and it is also suggestive of the __org__Seri Indian bird-skin robes made by sewing pelican skins together.

3. The word "brought" suggests that perhaps __person__Policarpio, like __person__Malquiares in the Ventureńo accounts, was an Indian crewman serving with __person__George Nidever on the voyage which brought __person__the woman off __place__the island (__person__Hudson __date__1978a: 26; __date__1978b: 152-153). Both __person__Dittman (__date__1973: 1) and __person__Nidever (__date__1973: 10) mention their crew as being composed of __org__Mission Indians. __person__Policarpio, a resident fisherman of the __place__Santa Barbara region at the time (and perhaps an experienced otter hunter) would certainly have been an ideal crewman. His death took place sometime __date__prior to 1856 (__person__Blackburn __date__1975: 19).

4. __person__Martina <emph>Lekte</emph> was a __person__Santa Cruz Island woman born at <emph>__place__Kaxas</emph> (__place__Prisoner's Harbor) and brought to the __place__mainland at __place__Santa Barbara while still a child. She was a niece of __person__Cecilia, the wife of <emph>__person__Kamuliyatset</emph>. __person__Martina was married twice and lived for a time in __place__Ventura (__person__Hudson __date__1979a: 149).

5. __person__Pilar, according to __person__Luisa Ygnacio, was first married to __person__Teofilo, a brother of __person__Policarpio. Thus __person__Pilar and __person__Luisa were sisters-in-law. Following Teofilo's death __person__Pilar married __person__Justo of __place__Ventura, a distant relative of <emph>Kamuliyatset's</emph> wife __person__Cecilia. Prior to Pilar's death __person__Justo fled to the __place__Thulare country with a Ventura woman named __person__Encelaria. __person__Pilar is buried at the __place__Mission Santa Barbara cemetery.

6. In __org__Ventureńo accounts __person__Harrington's informant reported that __person__the woman could understand __org__Fernandino (__person__Hudson __date__1978a: 25, 1978b: 153). We are now told that she was unable to understand the language. Possibly then, her language, __org__Nicoleńo, was distinct enough from __org__Fernandino so as not to be understood at all. A likely choice might be that __org__Nicoleńo was either a language of its own or a dialect of __org__Gabrielino spoken on __place__Santa Cruz Island. One of Harrington's Gabrielino informants, __person__Jose de los Santos Juncos, from __place__the mainland, stated that the __org__people of San Gabriel could not be understood by those of __place__Catalina Island (__person__Hudson __date__1979b: 361). It would therefore seem that __org__"Catalineńo" was a separate __org__Gabrielino language, as might also be the case for __org__Nicoleńo.
