THE EXCITEMENT OF DISCOVERY is just as rewarding in ethnography as in archaeology &#8212; and particularly so when one is digging through the exhaustive and overwhelming notes of __person__John P. Harrington. The total disorganization of the notes enhances the thrill of discovery, for one never knows what exciting and important piece of information awaits on the next handwritten page.

This recently happened to me as I was poring over Harrington's notes in Boxes 4 and 745 on the __org__Chumash Indians of __place__southern California. Box 4 dealt with Chumash material culture, while Box 745 contained mostly __org__Chumash ritual descriptions. It was quite a surprise to find among these notes material which pertained to the __person__"lone woman"* of San Nicolas Island.

*Also frequently known as the __person__"Lost Woman" &#8212; Ed.

The story of __person__this woman is well known, popularized by the romanticism of a Robinson Crusoe sort of existence (__date__1836?-1853) on a __place__tiny island off the __place__California coast. The important accounts of her story have been collected and published by __person__Heizer and __person__Elsasser (__date__1961); there is no need to repeat them here. Instead, Harrington's material will be presented, supplemented where necessary with end notes.

<hi rend="bold">A __place__San Nicolas Song</hi>
