About eighty miles southeast of Santa Barbara is a small island, some eight miles in length by three in breadth, named San Nicolas, which once supported a dense population. As artificers in stone and bone they excelled the inhabitants on the other islands, and those on the mainland. The finest specimens of aboriginal workmanship I have seen in California came from this island. But little is known of the people who inhabited San Nicolas island, except the records of the industry represented by these specimens. Owing to its distance from the mainland it seems not to have been discovered by Cabrillo, Vancouver, nor by any of the early or intermediate explorers. It was visited by the early American fur traders, however, and was called by them "Sea Otter Island." In the summer of 1835 a schooner was dispatched to this island to bring off the remnant of its inhabitants, now reduced to seventeen or eighteen individuals. The cause of their declination is enshrouded in as great mystery as that of the other islanders.

When all were supposed to be ready to sail, and the ship was about to weigh anchor, one of the women requested permission to return for her child, which she had left in a ca隳n nearby. But during her absence a heavy wind commenced blowing and drove the vessel from the island. In due time the schooner reached San Pedro in Los Angeles County, where the Indians were landed, and she was immediately ordered north by her owner, but in attempting to enter the Golden Gate, at San Francisco, was capsized and lost. There being no other large craft on this portion of the coast, no attempt was made to rescue this unfortunate woman. Indeed no effort was made to learn her fate for seventeen years afterward, although it was well known that she had been abandoned on the island. In 1851 the place was visited by a small schooner in quest of the eggs of waterfowl, when human tracks were discovered in the sand, which seemed to be those of a woman, but before search could be initiated a strong wind arose and drove the party from the inhospitable island. The following year the island was again visited by this party, who on going ashore found a large basket, woven from a species of grass, containing a gown made of bird's skins, a sinew rope, bone knife, fishhooks of shell, and other things, covered with the skin of a seal, but a second time they were driven from the island by the wind. In the summer of 1853 the island was visited for the third time by this party, who succeeded in making a thorough exploration, finding the object of their search seated in a low circular enclosure of sage brush. Within the enclosure was a smoldering fire, while without was a large pile of bones and another of ashes, showing this to have been her abode for several years. She was engaged in recovering the blubber from a seal skin, in which she employed a rude knife. She did not seem frightened, but met her rescuers with a smile and a bow. She wore a dress or gown made of bird skins, the feathers outside and pointing downward. Her hair was bleached by long exposure to a reddish brown, and hung down to her shoulders. Her features were regular and her complexion fairer than the Indians on the mainland. Though supposed to be about fifty years of age, her face was remarkably free from wrinkles. She willingly accompanied the party on board their vessel, and to the mainland. During her eighteen years of exile she seems to have entirely forgotten her language. Indians of various dialects were sent for, but none could understand her, nor she them. The change of diet to which she was subjected induced sickness, of which she died four weeks after reaching Santa Barbara. She made her rescuers understand by signs that she did not find her child but that it had been killed by dogs, a peculiar species that became extinct on this island but recently.
