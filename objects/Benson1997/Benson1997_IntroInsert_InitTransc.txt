 <hi rend="bold">MANUSCRIPT 6</hi>

[This fragment consists of pages 4 through 30 of what appears to be the first article in a series; Manuscript 7 (below) seems to be the second. Beginning with information taken from the journals of Juan Rodriguez de Cabrillo, the Portuguese explorer, and from various documents he found in mission archives, Bowers appears to have been searching for something that would shed light on the presumed disappearance of the Chumash. This initial section is followed by information obtained from Omset (probably pronounced Omtset), an old Indian from Santa Rosa Island. Omset told Bowers that his tribe was called Chumash. This name was later adopted to identify the people of the mainland coast, the Channel Islands, and the inland valleys who shared basic linguistic and cultural traits. Although Bowers consistently called these people Santa Barbara Indians, he apparently introduced the name Churnash into the literature. The next section of the manuscript consists of an account of the Lone Woman of San Nicolas Island, an Indian woman who was abandoned on this island for nearly 20 years. Most of the rest of the manuscript is devoted to descriptions of Chumash villages, with respect to aesthetics and defense. Finally, Bowers describes the "sweat lodge" of the California Indian, and includes a long and detailed description of the architectural features of a Pomo dance house and of a dance held within it. This description was copied by Bowers almost verbatim from a field notebook that is housed in the Special Collections Library at the Southwest Museum. It was Bowers' custom to copy his notes, often word for word, incorporating sections into finished manuscripts.]
