__org__These island people, judging by what we may learn of them now, led an almost ideal life. They made practical use of everything that came into their hands. All the bones of animals captured upon the land or in the sea were utilized, fashioned into awls, needles, tips for spears and other instruments for the chase or for domestic use. The methods employed by __org__these intelligent people in securing their food and preparing it were curious and interesting. A boulder from the beach would be selected for a mortar and the task of hollowing it out would be begun by taking a hard small stone in the hand and pecking away small particles of the boulder, fashioning it into the size and shape desired. Doubtless months of toil were required to make one of these vessels serviceable. Into the mortar, when it was completed, seeds of various kinds would be placed and ground to a powder, to be afterwards made into a dough, from which a kind of cake or tortilla was made. This was baked upon flat stones heated to white heat. The abalone, a species of shellfish, formed a staple srticle of food, but the flesh had to be pounded to a pulp to render it tender enough to eat.

__org__The islanders indulged in the smoking habit. Dried leaves were smoked in pipes which were beautifully wrought of stone into many different shapes. They produced fire by rubbing two sticks together.
	
Their method of fashioning arrow-heads and spear-points from the hard, flinty rock is remarkable. A piece of stone with cleavage was selected and a flake similar in size to the point desired chosen[.] The flake was laid in the palm of the hand[.] In the other hand the arrowmaker held a piece of hard bone ground to a point, and with a peculiar lateral pressure upon the edges, small pieces of the flint were chipped off with accuracy, and the desired shape given.

Hammers were made by taking a round stone and stretching over it a piece of the uncured skin of some animal, which also covered a portion of the short handle of wood. The instrument was put to dry in the sun, and when dry and the skin had been cut away from the two faces of the hammer it was ready for use. Cups were made of shells; fishhooks of two sharpened pieces of bone tied together with sinews. Beautiful ornaments were made of the bright-hued shells found on the beaches, and eating utensils were made from the same material, as well as from bones.
