<emph>__person__Sally Anne M. Thompson, Librarian at__org__St. Theresa School, __place__Phoenix, Arizona is the __date__1978 Chairman of the __org__Regina Medal Committee.</emph>

<hi rend="bold">I</hi>nternationally respected author __person__Scott O'Dell has been selected as the 20th recipient of the Regina Medal Award. In addition to this latest achievement, __person__O'Dell has received three Newbery Honor Awards (for <emph>__text__The King's Fifth</emph>, <emph>__text__The Black Pearl</emph> and <emph>__text__Sing Down the Moon</emph>); the Newbery Medal (for <emph>__text__Island of the Blue Dolphins</emph>); and in __date__1972 was the recipient of the Hans Christian Andersen Medal (awarded for the complete works of an author).

__person__Scott O'Dell's publisher records "we're proud of what he has given to the international children's book community, proud of the fine literature he has created for children&#8212;and very proud to be his publisher." It is worthy to note that __org__Houghton Mifflin Company has published <emph>all</emph> of O'Dell's children's books, beginning in __date__1960 with <emph>__text__Island of the Blue Dolphins</emph>.

Viewing the majority of his work, one discovers several qualities ever present. The quality woven into the grain of his rich tapestry is his own personal philosophy. No where does he overtly share innermost feelings or beliefs. These are felt or witnessed but not verbalized. From his writings though, we appreciate the fact that __person__O'Dell is an individual with a strong moral sense. He has spent his life intricately meshing bits of his own moral fiber into his books, and ultimately into the minds of his readers. His style is perceptive, complete with insights, as he skillfully creates depths of texture and fiber within his characters. __person__O'Dell expresses his point of view discretely through means of symbols and imagery, as manifested in __person__Tyndale's not allowing his life to be saved by the killing of another human being in <emph>__text__The Hawk That Dare Not Hunt By Day</emph>.

__person__The author is realistic in his approach to life. Good is not always rewarded&#8212;__person__Esteban was sentenced to five years in prison for his part in <emph>__text__The King's Fifth</emph>. Evil is not always punished&#8212;__person__Kit Carson pillaged, looted, and killed as viewed in <emph>__text__Sing Down the Moon</emph>, but became one of our nation's folk heroes. People do not always live happily ever after&#8212;__person__Caleb Clegg remained a haunted, driven man even after finding the ship's log that proved his innocence in <emph>__text__The Dark Canoe</emph>.

Woven throughout most of O'Dell's work is his kinship with the sea and his abiding love of __place__the Southwest. According to __person__the author: "my father was a railroad man so we moved a lot, but never far. Wherever we went, it was into frontier country like __place__Los Angeles . . . And __place__Rattlesnake Island, across the bay from __place__San Pedro, where we lived in a house on stilts and the waves came up and washed us under every day. And sailing ships went by. That is why, I suppose the feel of the frontier and the sound of the sea are in my books." The sea is constant in the <emph>__text__Island of the Blue Dolphins</emph>, in Roman's diving for <emph>__text__The Black Pearl</emph> off the __place__coast of Baja, with the __person__Clegg brothers out from __place__Nantucket in <emph>__text__The Dark Canoe</emph>, and as a vehicle for transporting Tyndale's valuable translations in <emph>__text__The Hawk That Dare Not Hunt By Day</emph>.

After a cruise with his wife __person__Elizabeth from __place__San Diego up to the __place__Columbia River on the ship __place__Artic Star, the <emph>__text__Cruise of the Artic Star</emph> evolved. Into this delightful book is spun the texture of the cruise and the "frontier." It is decorated and personalized with characters relevant to the historical and geographical ports of call along the way.

The best in historical fiction is consistently exemplified by __person__O'Dell. His stories detail qualities which insure satisfaction for the reader and lasting interest in his books. Historical events, facts and characters recapture the flavor of the time, captivating the reader by allowing him to enter a world of romantic appeal, totally encompassed in authentic language and custom. Yet, the historical data does not appear as cold facts as in a documented commentary, but comes alive as warm essentials.

__person__O'Dell's stories cause the reader to ponder the effects people have upon each other; the character, conduct, morality and ethical values; enabling one to see that human nature was the same then as now. Picturesque language, scrupulously accurate details such as a church that took three generations to build, all add dimension to the authenticity of the stories.

Readers are helped to understand the past, what the struggle of man has been and why people have acted as they have. "Children don't relate to names of battles, dates of treaties, to statistics. Like us, they relate to individuals, to emotions they can feel within themselves, to stories that arouse their curiosity," said __person__O'Dell.

Perspective and balance are part of the lesson of history and the thoughtful reader will see this reflected in the problems and issues of each era __person__O'Dell covers, from the __date__Civil War period to <emph>__text__The 290</emph>, the whaling days of <emph>__text__The Dark Canoe</emph>, the explorations of __org__the Spanish of <emph>__text__The King's Fifth</emph>, to the realization that the __org__United States Army did march the __org__Navajos to __place__Fort Sumner as did __person__Bright Morning in <emph>__text__Sing Down the Moon</emph>.

__person__O' Dell uses history to his advantage. He wrote me that "<emph>__text__The Hawk That Dare Not Hunt By Day</emph> was very difficult to write because of the material and technical matters. I want to acquaint young people with the origins of the language they speak. Also to remind them that there are men whom they can emulate to advantage."

__person__The master weaver weaves his ideas of human relationships as evidenced in the close family bond between parents, child and grandmother in <emph>__text__Journey to Jericho</emph>; man's place in the universal scheme, witnessed in __person__Karana's fight for survival; the oft lamented theme of man's inhumanity to man in the brutality and cruelty measured out to the ___org___Indians three hundred years ago by __org__the Conquistadors in <emph>__text__The King's Fifth</emph>.

<hi rend="bold">A</hi>s the reader relates to __person__O'Dell's characters he can come to grips with himself, gain an understanding of self. The characters such as __person__Tom, __person__Karana and __person__Esteban embody the hopes and ideals of the reader. This has appeal as a source of challenge and enjoyment. Part of this lies in having one's imagination stimulated. __person__O'Dell breathes life into his characters with painstaking craftsmanship; flesh and blood personalities. Characters that are real, that live and move in a rotating world. Characters that are not static, but individuals that are ever growing, changing and unique in their actions. They live their lives as the author sees them, realistically portraying the whole spectrum of human life with no quick and easy solutions to serious problems. The convictions of __person__the author are so skillfully tied into the pattern of the work that the individual fibers are taken for granted. __person__The author hopes that the reader is capable of formulating a set of values and a sense of responsibility through understanding the complexities of concepts of truth and honesty, as depicted by specific situations and vicarious experiences that give insight into the characters groping to deal with their problems.

__person__O'Dell, with rare talent, is able to accurately depict characters through revealing speech and incident. He does not tell his reader how life is, or how people are capable of behaving, he merely creates the situations that force his characters to be themselves.

He has not subjected his reader to stereotypes that have been common especially in books written __date__before the mid 1970's. Instead, his females are respected as individuals, active participants with characteristics suitable to their roles. In <emph>__text__The King's Fifth</emph>, the Indian girl __person__Zia acts as guide and interpreter for the __org__Spanish explorers; __person__Karana embodies all the qualities one could possibly achieve as she survives her __date__eighteen year isolation on the <emph>__text__Island of the Blue Dolphins</emph>.

O'Dell stories entertain the reader, hold his attention and make him care about the dilemmas of the characters. The reader can vicariously experience the defeats and victories of each character. Perhaps this is because he writes about situations pertinent to the human condition; the constant battle between good and evil [is] obvious even to the pre-adolescent reader. __person__O'Dell is capable of subtly and skillfully weaving this message between the written lines of his stories, much as the Navajo weaves her rug, thread by thread, to form a complete and beautiful expression of herself.

<emph>__text__Child of Fire</emph> portrays the seriousness of brutality as a way of life. But the grim details are used for a purpose; to give the reader as accurate a picture as possible of the events taking place in the story and of its setting. __person__O'Dell has given the reader what has meaning for him. He offered the following explanation to my inquiry about this work: ". . . I've been a director, in __place__San Diego, of an organization called __org__Crash, Inc. We're funded by the __org__State and __org__County and deal with __org__Chicanos and __org__blacks in several experimental houses, __org__Tranquility Home, __org__Halfway House, and others. I know these kids pretty well. What I've written is an attempt to present their problems, how they solve them for themselves, and what, if anything, can be done about their solutions. The book was a celebration of the __org__Chicano spirit, rather than a put down."

__person__Scott O'Dell has made a memorilble contribution to the world of children's literature. At an age when most people are secure in relaxed, comfortable retirement, the __person__master weaver is busily working at his loom, researching and writing. <emph>__text__CarIota</emph>, published in __date__1977, is his most recent contribution. His writings have and will stand the test of time, because his message is so potent and accurate that it speaks to all generations.

Thus does __person__Scott O'Dell, master weaver, qualify for __person__ Walter de Ia Mare's quote ". . . only the rarest kind of best in anything can be good enough for the young."
