The San Francisco journals contain an account of what they call a California <i>Crusoe</i>, an Indian woman who had, for 18 years, lived alone on a dreary desert island, and was finally discovered and taken off by the crew of a vessel in search of her. Many years ago a small schooner was sent to the Island of San Nicholas in the Pacific, some 75 miles sout-east [south-east] of Santa Barbara, to bring away a number of Indians living there and settle them on the mainland. Nineteen men, woman, and children had been got on board, when one of them, a mother, found that two of her offspring had been left behind. She immediately jumped overboard and swam to the island, where she sought in vain for her children. Having returned to the shore, she saw the schooner sailing away, and tried in vain to attract attention. The island was not visited again for 16 years. Then GEORGE NIDEVER, an otter-hunter commanding a small vessel, landed there, and detected evidences of human habitation, but could not stay long enough to prosecute his quest. Two years after, he smiled there again, and, roaming over the island, came upon the woman, who was not at all wild, and made no efforts to escape. She was clad in a garment fashioned of the skin of a sea-fowl, and was occupied in skinning seal-blubber, which had formed the greater part of her diet. She was quite good-looking, seemed about 50 years old, and spoke a language nobody could understand. She dies a few weeks after reaching Santa Barbara, while living in the house of NIDEVER, from the effects of a fall seriously injuring her spine. Her death was much mourned by several enterprising showmen in San Francisco, who had offered handsome sums for the privilege of exhibiting her through the county as the Unique and Entirely Wonderful California <i>Crusoe</i>.
